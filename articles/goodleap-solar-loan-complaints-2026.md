<h2>What GoodLeap Actually Is</h2>

<p>GoodLeap is a California-based lender that finances residential solar installations. Functionally, it works like this: a solar installer (Sunrun, Freedom Forever, Momentum Solar, Vivint, thousands of smaller dealers) sells you a system. The installer is a "dealer" in GoodLeap's network. GoodLeap underwrites and funds the loan. You sign the loan documents (often on a tablet). GoodLeap pays the installer directly — meaning you never see the money. You pay GoodLeap back over 10, 15, 20, or 25 years.</p>

<p>The structure is straightforward. The problems start in the details.</p>

<h2>The Four GoodLeap Complaint Patterns</h2>

<h3>Pattern #1: Hidden Dealer Fees Inflating the Loan Principal</h3>

<p>This is the big one. GoodLeap's business model involves "dealer fees" — commissions paid from GoodLeap to the installer for bringing them the loan. Industry reporting and consumer complaints consistently describe these dealer fees running 15-30% of the loan amount.</p>

<p>Here is the issue: if the true cash price of a solar system is $30,000 and GoodLeap charges a 25% dealer fee, the loan principal becomes $40,000 — but that extra $10,000 is not a finance charge disclosed as interest. It is bundled into the stated "cost of the system." The result: the stated APR on your loan document looks reasonable, but the <strong>true cost of credit</strong> is significantly higher than disclosed.</p>

<p>This is exactly what the NY AG's March 17, 2026 lawsuit against Attyx/SUNco alleges against Solar Mosaic and WebBank — they are named as defendants for "concealing the lenders' fees by hiding them within the high prices the consumers were supposedly paying." (<a href="/ny-ag-attyx-sunco-275-million-lawsuit-2026">Full NY AG breakdown</a>.) Truth in Lending Act (TILA) violations apply to these structures and they open the door to contract rescission. (<a href="/goodleap-dealer-fees-explained">Dealer fees explained</a>.)</p>

<h3>Pattern #2: "Prepay and Save" Payment Structures That Backfire</h3>

<p>Many GoodLeap loans are structured with an 18-month "introductory" period where payments are calculated as if you received the 30% federal solar tax credit and applied it to the principal. If you do not apply the tax credit (or never qualified for it), your monthly payment jumps — sometimes by 50% or more — in month 19.</p>

<p>The problem: solar salespeople routinely told homeowners they qualified for the tax credit when they did not (retirees with no tax liability, people on fixed incomes). Month 19 arrives. Payment jumps from $218 to $342. The homeowner calls GoodLeap. GoodLeap says the loan is what it is and points at the installer. The installer has vanished, been sold, or gone bankrupt.</p>

<h3>Pattern #3: UCC Liens on Your Home</h3>

<p>Many GoodLeap loans include a UCC-1 financing statement filed against the solar equipment. In practice, this can show up in title searches when you sell your home — sometimes showing up as a cloud on your title that a buyer's title company flags. You have to discharge the lien, which means paying off the full remaining loan balance in cash, to clear the title.</p>

<p>GoodLeap's position is the UCC lien is "on the equipment, not the home." Title companies frequently disagree. (<a href="/solar-lien-on-my-house">Full solar lien breakdown</a>.)</p>

<h3>Pattern #4: Service Issues When the Installer Goes Under</h3>

<p>When Sunnova went bankrupt. When SunPower went bankrupt. When Freedom Forever filed Chapter 11 on April 15, 2026. When your original installer disappears and warranty/service obligations become uncertain — <strong>GoodLeap continues billing regardless.</strong></p>

<p>GoodLeap's position: "Your loan with us is separate from your contract with the installer." Technically true. Practically problematic — because you bought a solar <em>system</em>, not a <em>loan</em>, and when the system stops working or the warranty is repudiated, the loan loses its underlying purpose.</p>

<p>This is exactly where the FTC Holder Rule becomes your best friend. More on that below.</p>

<h2>The Legal Theories — Why GoodLeap Loans Are More Cancellable Than You Think</h2>

<h3>Theory #1: TILA Rescission</h3>

<p>The Truth in Lending Act gives consumers a 3-year rescission right in transactions secured by their principal dwelling when the lender violates specific disclosure requirements. Hidden dealer fees that distort the APR disclosure are a classic TILA violation. If the UCC lien language in your GoodLeap loan qualifies as a security interest in your home, TILA rescission may be available for up to 3 years after closing.</p>

<h3>Theory #2: FTC Holder Rule</h3>

<p>Every consumer credit contract financing a retail purchase must include (or be deemed to include) the FTC Holder Rule notice: "ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES…"</p>

<p>In plain English: when you have a claim against Sunrun, Freedom Forever, or any solar installer, that claim also runs against GoodLeap. If the installer misrepresented the system, GoodLeap is on the hook. If the warranty is repudiated, GoodLeap is on the hook. If the system underperforms, GoodLeap is on the hook. Full loan cancellation via Holder Rule is a realistic outcome. (<a href="/cancel-goodleap-loan">How to cancel a GoodLeap loan</a>.)</p>

<h3>Theory #3: State Consumer Protection Law</h3>

<p>State Deceptive Trade Practices Acts — Texas DTPA, California CLRA/UCL, NY GBL §349, Florida FDUTPA, Connecticut UTPA — apply to lenders that participate in fraudulent schemes. When GoodLeap funds a loan tied to a misrepresented solar sale, they are potentially liable under the same consumer protection statutes that apply to the installer.</p>

<h3>Theory #4: CFPB Supervisory Leverage</h3>

<p>GoodLeap is a large nonbank consumer lender and is subject to CFPB supervision. Individual CFPB complaints create regulatory paper trails that pressure GoodLeap to resolve individual cases quickly rather than let them accumulate into supervisory concerns.</p>

## FAQ Schema

### What are the most common GoodLeap solar loan complaints?

Four patterns dominate: hidden dealer fees inflating the loan principal by 15-30% above the true system cost, 18-month introductory payment structures that jump sharply in month 19 when homeowners cannot apply the federal tax credit, UCC-1 liens on the equipment that can cloud home titles during resale, and continued billing after the original installer goes bankrupt or stops honoring warranty obligations. The FTC Holder Rule, TILA rescission, and state consumer protection laws provide legal paths to address all four.

### Can I cancel my GoodLeap solar loan?

Yes, through four legal theories: (1) Truth in Lending Act rescission, available for up to 3 years after closing when hidden dealer fees distorted the disclosed APR; (2) the FTC Holder Rule, which makes GoodLeap subject to the same claims you have against the solar installer — particularly useful when the installer went bankrupt or misrepresented the sale; (3) state consumer protection statutes that apply to lenders participating in fraudulent schemes; and (4) CFPB complaints that create supervisory leverage. Full loan cancellation is a realistic outcome for strong cases.

### Why did my GoodLeap payment jump in month 19?

Your loan was almost certainly structured with an 18-month 'introductory' period that assumed you would apply the 30% federal solar tax credit to the principal. If you did not apply the credit — either because you did not qualify (retirees or low-tax-liability homeowners often do not) or because nobody told you this step was required — your payment recalculates based on the full principal starting in month 19. The increase can be 50% or more. This is the single most common trigger for homeowners realizing they have a GoodLeap problem.

### Does GoodLeap put a lien on my house?

GoodLeap files a UCC-1 financing statement covering the solar equipment in most cases. Technically this is a lien on the equipment, not the home. However, title companies frequently flag the UCC filing during a home sale, requiring the homeowner to pay off the full remaining loan balance in cash to discharge the lien before closing. In practice, a UCC lien functions as a cloud on title that can block or complicate a home sale.

### What is the FTC Holder Rule and how does it apply to my GoodLeap loan?

The FTC Holder Rule requires every consumer credit contract to include (or be deemed to include) notice that the lender is subject to all claims and defenses the consumer could assert against the seller. In plain English: if the solar installer misrepresented the system, if the warranty is not being honored, or if the system underperforms, those claims also apply to GoodLeap. This is the strongest legal theory for loan cancellation when the original installer has gone bankrupt or committed fraud — you do not need to sue the installer first.
