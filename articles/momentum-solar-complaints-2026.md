<h2>The Documented Complaint Pattern</h2>

<p>Three patterns dominate the Momentum Solar complaint record across BBB, CFPB, and state Attorney General consumer protection databases:</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 1: Production well below sales pitch.</strong> Customers report actual production numbers 20 to 40 percent below what was projected during the sale. Momentum's standard pitch promises specific monthly savings tied to a system sized for the homeowner's average usage. When the system underperforms — because it was undersized, oriented poorly, or shaded by trees that were not assessed — the math the sale was based on collapses. The homeowner is left with both an electric bill AND a solar payment.</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 2: Warranty calls that go nowhere.</strong> The recurring complaint: customer calls about a non-producing inverter, a panel that is offline, monitoring data that stopped reporting. First call gets a ticket number. Follow-up calls are routed to different reps with no record of the prior conversation. Service trucks scheduled but not dispatched. Issues unresolved for 6, 12, 18 months. This is consistent with what you would expect from a company growing faster than its service infrastructure.</p>

<p><strong>Pattern 3: Sales misrepresentation about tax credit and savings.</strong> The 30 percent federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is not a guarantee — it depends on the homeowner's federal tax liability. Many Momentum customers report being told they would "definitely get the full credit back," when in fact their tax situation made them ineligible for the full benefit. Combined with savings projections that did not pan out, this is the textbook misrepresentation pattern that supports common-law fraudulent inducement and state UDAP claims.</p>

<h2>Your Legal Options</h2>

<p>The strongest cases for Momentum Solar customers stack three or four claims:</p>

<h3>1. FTC Holder Rule against the lender (16 C.F.R. § 433.2)</h3>

<p>If Momentum's salesperson misrepresented production, savings, or the tax credit, those claims flow to the lender (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Service Finance) under the FTC Holder Rule. Recovery is capped at amounts paid, but in practice this often equals the remaining loan balance, producing full loan cancellation.</p>

<h3>2. Common-law fraudulent inducement</h3>

<p>Available in every state. The five elements: material misrepresentation of fact, knowledge of falsity (or reckless disregard), intent to induce reliance, justifiable reliance, damages. "You will save $X per year" backed by no realistic basis is textbook misrepresentation when production data later shows the projection was unsupportable.</p>

<h3>3. State UDAP (varies by state)</h3>

<p>Each state has different leverage:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Texas:</strong> DTPA — treble damages plus attorney's fees. The Texas AG investigation of Sunrun and Freedom Forever (announced April 6, 2026) provides supporting framework that consumer attorneys are now using for Momentum cases too. (See the <a href="/texas-ag-sunrun-solar-investigation-2026">Texas AG breakdown</a>.)</li>
<li><strong>California:</strong> CLRA + UCL — actual damages, restitution, injunction, attorney's fees. Plus B&P 7031 if Momentum's California license was invalid for any portion of the install.</li>
<li><strong>New York:</strong> GBL 349 and 350 — treble damages capped at $1,000, plus attorney's fees. NY AG is actively pursuing solar fraud post-Attyx.</li>
<li><strong>New Jersey:</strong> Consumer Fraud Act — mandatory treble damages plus attorney's fees. Momentum has heavy NJ presence; this is the strongest single-state UDAP claim.</li>
<li><strong>Florida:</strong> FDUTPA — actual damages plus attorney's fees. Multiple class actions filed.</li>
<li><strong>Pennsylvania:</strong> UTPCPL — minimum $100 plus actual damages and attorney's fees, treble in egregious cases.</li>
</ul>

<h3>4. Hidden dealer fee attack</h3>

<p>If your Momentum loan includes a dealer fee (it almost certainly does), the dealer fee creates additional TILA disclosure violations — the markup arguably should have been disclosed as a finance charge under 15 U.S.C. § 1605, not bundled into "Amount Financed." Material TILA defects extend rescission rights from 3 days to 3 years under 15 U.S.C. § 1635.</p>

<h3>5. Material breach (warranty failure)</h3>

<p>If you have documented unanswered warranty calls, denied service requests, and ongoing equipment problems, that is material breach. Material breach excuses further performance and gives a claim for damages — and under the Holder Rule, it flows to the lender.</p>

## FAQ

### Can I cancel a Momentum Solar contract if my system isn't producing what they promised?

Yes — production below sales projection is one of the strongest grounds for cancellation. Pull 12 months of monitoring data and compare to the savings projection in your original sales proposal. A shortfall of 20% or more typically supports common-law fraudulent inducement (the projection was a misrepresentation made without realistic basis) and state UDAP claims. Under the FTC Holder Rule (16 CFR 433.2), these claims also flow to your lender — GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, or Service Finance — making the lender liable for the misrepresentation. Stacked with warranty failure claims (material breach) and dealer fee misrepresentation, the combination often produces full loan cancellation with the equipment retained on the roof. The strongest cases combine 3-4 of these legal theories simultaneously.

### Is Momentum Solar going bankrupt?

Momentum Solar has not filed bankruptcy as of April 2026, but the financial pressure on residential solar installers is intense. Sunnova, SunPower, Solar Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, and Freedom Forever (April 15, 2026 Chapter 11) all filed in the last 24 months. The same forces apply to Momentum: declining residential installations, OBBBA tax credit reductions, rising customer service costs from earlier installations, and accumulating legal liability. Customers who document their cases now will be in dramatically stronger position if Momentum does file than customers starting from scratch after news of a filing breaks. Bankruptcy actually creates additional legal grounds (material breach + FTC Holder Rule attack on the lender) — it does not foreclose cancellation, it strengthens it.

### What states have the strongest legal claims against Momentum Solar?

Five states currently have particularly strong leverage: (1) Texas — DTPA treble damages, plus the Texas AG investigation of Sunrun/Freedom Forever provides supporting framework consumer attorneys use for Momentum cases too; (2) New Jersey — Consumer Fraud Act mandates treble damages plus attorney's fees, and Momentum has heavy NJ presence; (3) California — CLRA, UCL, plus B&P 7031 if installer license was invalid; (4) Arizona — Consumer Fraud Act covers Momentum's heavy Phoenix/Tucson activity; (5) New York — GBL 349/350 treble damages, plus the NY AG is actively pursuing solar fraud post-Attyx. All states have UDAP statutes that reach Momentum's pattern of conduct, but the five above offer the strongest combination of mandatory damages multipliers, attorney fee shifting, and AG enforcement support.

### Does my Momentum Solar loan have a hidden dealer fee?

Almost certainly yes. Momentum Solar primarily routes loans through GoodLeap, Solar Mosaic (now in Chapter 11), Sunlight Financial (bankrupt 2024), and Service Finance — all of which the CFPB documented as charging hidden dealer fees in its August 2024 Issue Spotlight, and three of which (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, plus Dividend) are defendants in the Minnesota AG's March 2024 lawsuit. Dealer fees typically range from 10-36% of the cash price, added directly to your loan principal but not disclosed as a finance charge. To verify: request the cash price of your system from Momentum in writing — the difference between the cash price and your loan principal is the dealer fee. If Momentum refuses to disclose the cash price, that refusal is itself evidence supporting the dealer fee misrepresentation claim. The dealer fee creates additional TILA disclosure violations that extend rescission rights from 3 days to 3 years.

### What should I do first if I have problems with Momentum Solar?

Document, then act. Pull every relevant document immediately: the sales proposal (the savings projection is your most important single document), contract, financing agreement, monitoring portal login, warranty card, and any written communications. Pull 12 months of monitoring data and compare to the sales projection — quantify the production shortfall. Document every warranty call or email — date, time, person, ticket number, response received or lack thereof. Identify your lender (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, or Service Finance) — they are who you will be filing the FTC Holder Rule claim against, not Momentum. Calculate the dealer fee by requesting the cash price in writing. Once documentation is complete, file a formal Holder Rule claim against the lender and a parallel UDAP claim against Momentum directly under your state's deceptive trade practices statute. Do NOT stop paying the loan without legal cover — coordinate any payment changes with your filed claim.
