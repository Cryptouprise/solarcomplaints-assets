<h2>What a Sunrun Solar Lease Actually Is</h2>

<p>A Sunrun lease (or PPA — Power Purchase Agreement — they are functionally similar) is a 20 or 25-year contract where Sunrun owns the panels on your roof and you pay Sunrun monthly for the electricity those panels produce. You do not own the system. You cannot deduct anything on your taxes. You cannot take the federal tax credit — Sunrun does. You cannot transfer it easily when you sell your house.</p>

<p>What you are buying is <em>the right to consume electricity from panels on your own roof</em>. That is the pitch. Now let's look at the mechanisms.</p>

<h2>The Five Mechanisms That Make a Sunrun Lease Work for Sunrun, Not You</h2>

<h3>Mechanism #1: The 2.9% Annual Escalator</h3>

<p>This is the big one. Most Sunrun leases include an annual escalator clause — typically 2.9% per year — that increases your monthly payment automatically. It is presented by the sales rep (if it is mentioned at all) as "just keeping pace with utility rate inflation."</p>

<p>It does not. Here's the math:</p>

<ul>
<li>Year 1 payment: $150/month</li>
<li>Year 5: $168/month</li>
<li>Year 10: $194/month</li>
<li>Year 15: $224/month</li>
<li>Year 20: $258/month</li>
<li>Year 25: $298/month</li>
</ul>

<p>That $150/month you signed for? You are paying $298/month by the end. <strong>Total paid over 25 years: roughly $65,000 — on a system that would have cost $25,000-$35,000 to buy outright with cash.</strong></p>

<p>Meanwhile, utility electricity rates have historically increased at roughly 2-3% annually nationwide, but many markets have been lower, some have been flat, and in states with solar-hostile policy changes (California's NEM 3.0, Indiana, Arizona, Nevada), rooftop solar value has actually decreased. The escalator is not hedging you against utility inflation — it is guaranteed revenue growth for Sunrun, regardless of what utility rates actually do. (<a href="/solar-contract-escalator-clause-explained">Full breakdown</a>.)</p>

<h3>Mechanism #2: The 25-Year Lock-In</h3>

<p>The average American homeowner moves every 13 years. The average Sunrun lease is 25 years. Do the math.</p>

<p>When you try to sell your house before the lease ends, you have three options, all bad:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Buy the lease out in cash.</strong> Sunrun uses a "present value" formula that is deliberately difficult to predict. Buyouts for 10-year-old systems frequently come in at $25,000-$40,000 — for equipment that would cost maybe $8,000 used.</li>
<li><strong>Transfer the lease to the buyer.</strong> Requires the buyer to credit-qualify with Sunrun (many don't), and a lot of buyers simply refuse to assume a 25-year obligation tied to your roof. (<a href="/trapped-in-solar-lease-selling-house">Trapped in a solar lease when selling your house</a>.)</li>
<li><strong>Walk away.</strong> Sunrun can sue for the remaining lease value — often $50,000-$100,000 in projected payments.</li>
</ol>

<p>This is not a bug. This is the business model. Sunrun's market cap depends on locked-in 25-year revenue streams, and the contract is engineered to keep you in.</p>

<h3>Mechanism #3: The "Present Value" Buyout Formula</h3>

<p>Your lease has a buyout option. The math behind it is buried in an appendix in terms most homeowners cannot parse. The result is buyouts that are consistently higher than the resale value of the equipment, by design.</p>

<p>Sunrun determines the buyout by calculating the present value of your remaining projected payments — which are themselves inflated by the 2.9% escalator. So you are paying in advance for future payments that were already marked up. It is a second tax on trying to exit.</p>

<h3>Mechanism #4: Production Guarantees That Aren't Really Guarantees</h3>

<p>Sunrun markets a production guarantee — if the system produces less than promised, Sunrun will reimburse you. In practice, the reimbursement calculations are opaque, the threshold for triggering the guarantee is often so far below "what the sales rep promised" that the guarantee never activates, and when it does, the reimbursement is a tiny fraction of what you actually lost on your utility bill.</p>

<p>Worse: production guarantees are among the first things to get voided in bankruptcy.
