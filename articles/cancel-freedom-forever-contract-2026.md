<h2>What the Bankruptcy Actually Means for You</h2>

<p>Chapter 11 is reorganization bankruptcy. Freedom Forever is not shutting down immediately — it is restructuring while continuing limited operations. But the practical reality for customers is that warranty claims, service requests, and production guarantee obligations are now moving through bankruptcy court rather than being handled normally.</p>

<p>Three things matter:</p>

<p><strong>1. Your loan does not automatically disappear.</strong> The loan is between you and the lender (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, etc.). Freedom Forever's bankruptcy does not extinguish that loan. You are still legally on the hook for monthly payments unless and until you successfully attack the loan through one of the legal mechanisms below.</p>

<p>If you simply stop paying without filing a formal legal challenge, the lender will report you to credit bureaus, the loan will go to collections, and you can be sued. Stopping payments is a strategic move that should be coordinated with a documented legal claim. Do not stop paying without legal cover.</p>

<p><strong>2. Your warranty is functionally dead even if it is technically alive.</strong> Freedom Forever's customer-facing obligations — production guarantees, equipment warranties, service contracts — are "executory contracts" under 11 U.S.C. § 365. The bankrupt entity has the right to "reject" these contracts as part of restructuring. When that happens, your warranty becomes a pre-petition unsecured claim against the bankruptcy estate, which historically pays out at 5 to 15 cents on the dollar.</p>

<p>Even before formal rejection, the practical reality is that warranty calls go unanswered. Service trucks do not show up. Production guarantee credits do not appear. This is the same pattern that played out with Sunnova and SunPower in 2024, and is currently playing out with SunStrong's refusal to honor those warranties (which the Connecticut AG is now investigating — see <a href="/sunstrong-warranty-not-honored-sunnova-sunpower-2026">the SunStrong breakdown</a>).</p>

<p><strong>3. The FTC Holder Rule is the lever.</strong> Under 16 C.F.R. § 433.2, your lender is liable for the installer's misconduct, up to amounts you have already paid. Freedom Forever's failure to deliver the contracted product (working system, honored warranty, production guarantee) is misconduct. The lender inherits the claim. This is the path that produces actual loan reduction or cancellation.</p>

<h2>The Strongest Legal Claims for Freedom Forever Customers</h2>

<p>Multiple paths apply. The strongest cases stack three or four together.</p>

<h3>Path 1: FTC Holder Rule + Material Breach</h3>

<p>The mechanics: Freedom Forever's bankruptcy is evidence that they cannot perform the contract going forward. The warranty is functionally void. The production guarantee is functionally void. The service obligations are functionally void. That is material breach. Under the FTC Holder Rule, breach claims flow from the installer to the lender. Your lender becomes liable for what Freedom Forever can no longer deliver.</p>

<p>Recovery is capped at amounts paid (under the federal Holder Rule), but in practice this is often enough to cancel the remaining loan balance entirely. Many cases settle with the lender writing off the balance and reporting the loan as paid in full to credit bureaus.</p>

<h3>Path 2: Common-Law Fraudulent Inducement</h3>

<p>If your Freedom Forever salesperson made specific promises about savings, system performance, or company longevity that turned out to be false, you have a fraudulent inducement claim. Common law in every state. Five elements: material misrepresentation of fact, knowledge of falsity (or reckless disregard), intent to induce reliance, justifiable reliance, damages.</p>

<p>"Freedom Forever has been around forever and will always be there to honor your warranty" is a textbook misrepresentation when made within months of a Chapter 11 filing. Sales reps making this kind of statement in 2024 or 2025 — when financial distress was already documented in industry trade press — establishes the intent element.</p>

<h3>Path 3: Texas DTPA (for Texas customers)</h3>

<p>Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton announced an investigation of Freedom Forever (and Sunrun) on April 6, 2026, just days before the Chapter 11 filing. The investigation is being conducted under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (Bus. & Com. Code § 17.50). Texas customers have particularly strong leverage right now because the AG investigation provides documentary support for individual cases. (See the <a href="/texas-ag-sunrun-solar-investigation-2026">full Texas AG investigation breakdown</a>.)</p>

<p>DTPA awards treble damages plus attorney's fees for knowing violations. A fraudulent inducement claim under DTPA, stacked with the Holder Rule attack on the lender, is the dominant settlement framework for Texas Freedom Forever customers right now.</p>

<h2>What NOT to Do</h2>

<p>Three mistakes that destroy cases:</p>

<p><strong>Do not stop paying your loan without legal cover.</strong> Stopping payments triggers default, credit damage, and potential lawsuits from the lender. Stopping payments AS PART OF A DOCUMENTED legal claim is a different matter — but the documentation has to come first. Continue payments while the case is being built.</p>

<p><strong>Do not communicate with Freedom Forever's bankruptcy claims processor without understanding what you are signing.</strong> The bankruptcy estate will send claim forms. Some include releases or waivers that, if signed, eliminate your right to pursue separate claims against the lender under the FTC Holder Rule. Read every form carefully or have someone read it for you. The general rule: filing a proof of claim in the bankruptcy is fine; signing a settlement or release is not, until you understand the trade-off.</p>

<p><strong>Do not accept a "settlement" from the lender that does not include credit reporting cleanup.</strong> Some lenders will offer to "release" the remaining balance in exchange for the customer dropping all claims, but report the loan as "settled for less than full balance" to credit bureaus. That credit notation hurts the customer for years. Any settlement should include the lender reporting the loan as "paid in full" — which is the standard outcome in successful Holder Rule cases.</p>

## FAQ

### Does Freedom Forever's bankruptcy automatically cancel my loan?

No. Your loan is between you and the lender (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, Service Finance, or Dividend) — not with Freedom Forever directly. Freedom Forever's Chapter 11 bankruptcy does not extinguish that loan. However, the bankruptcy creates strong legal grounds to attack the loan through the FTC Holder Rule (16 CFR 433.2), which makes the lender liable for the installer's material breach. The bankruptcy is evidence that Freedom Forever cannot perform warranty, production guarantee, and service obligations going forward — that breach flows to the lender under the Holder Rule. Successful cases typically result in full loan cancellation with equipment retained, but require documented legal action, not just stopping payments.

### Should I stop paying my Freedom Forever loan now that they filed bankruptcy?

Not without documented legal cover first. Simply stopping payments triggers default, credit damage, and potential lender lawsuits — without giving you any defensive position. The correct sequence: (1) collect all documents (contract, financing docs, monitoring data, warranty claim records); (2) document Freedom Forever's failure to perform (unanswered service calls, denied warranty claims, underproduction vs. guarantee); (3) file a formal legal claim against the lender under the FTC Holder Rule asserting Freedom Forever's breach; (4) THEN, with your case documented, you have negotiating leverage. Continue payments during the case-building phase. Stopping payments without legal cover destroys your position; stopping payments AS PART OF a documented Holder Rule case is legitimate strategy.

### What is the deadline to file a claim in the Freedom Forever bankruptcy?

The bankruptcy court will issue a 'bar date' — typically 90 to 180 days from the April 15, 2026 filing date. Customers who miss the bar date lose their right to participate in the bankruptcy distribution for warranty claims, production guarantee shortfalls, and service obligations. However, your FTC Holder Rule claim against the LENDER (not Freedom Forever) is separate and not subject to the bankruptcy bar date — that claim runs against the lender directly under federal regulation. The practical timeline: pull documents within 30 days, file the Holder Rule claim within 60-90 days, and meet whatever bar date the bankruptcy court sets for the parallel bankruptcy claim against Freedom Forever's estate.

### What if I'm a Freedom Forever lease customer, not a loan customer?

Lease customers have a parallel but different path. The lessor is usually a special-purpose investor entity (often named something like 'FFI Solar Holdings 2022-A LLC') — not Freedom Forever directly. The lessor inherits obligations to maintain the system, honor the production guarantee, and provide service. When the lessor cannot perform because their service partner (Freedom Forever) is bankrupt, the lease is breached. Lease customers should: (1) identify the lessor on monthly bills; (2) document service request failures; (3) pull production data showing underperformance vs. guarantee; (4) notify the lessor in writing of breach (triggers the lessor's cure period); (5) if the lessor cannot cure, the lease becomes voidable, often allowing the homeowner to keep the equipment in place at no further cost. Lease cases take longer than loan cases but do work, especially under CLRA/UCL in California, GBL 349 in New York, and other strong UDAP frameworks.

### How does the Texas AG investigation affect my Freedom Forever case?

Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton announced a formal investigation of Freedom Forever and Sunrun on April 6, 2026 (nine days before Freedom Forever's Chapter 11 filing) under the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act. For Texas customers, this is significant: (1) the AG investigation generates discovery that supports individual cases; (2) Texas DTPA awards treble damages plus attorney's fees for knowing violations; (3) consumer attorneys can use the AG investigation as documentary foundation, reducing the cost of building individual cases; (4) lenders typically settle Texas DTPA claims more favorably during active AG investigations to avoid being named in expanded actions. Texas Freedom Forever customers have particularly strong leverage right now compared to customers in states without active investigations. This window typically lasts 6-12 months from the AG announcement.
