<h2>How to Read This Guide</h2>

<p>Each of the 14 loopholes below has three parts: <strong>what the law actually says</strong>, <strong>when it applies to a solar contract</strong>, and <strong>what to do if it might apply to yours</strong>. Several are federal and apply everywhere in the U.S. Some are state-specific and most powerful in California, New York, Texas, Florida, Connecticut, and Massachusetts.</p>

<p>The numbers are ranked roughly by power and likelihood of application, not by legal sophistication. #1 is the nuke. #14 is a niche angle that's sometimes exactly right.</p>

<h2>#1 — The FTC Holder Rule (16 C.F.R. § 433.2)</h2>

<p>If you financed a solar system through a lender that the installer referred you to — GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, Dividend, Service Finance, GreenSky — your loan documents are required to contain a specific notice. That notice says, in bold type:</p>

<blockquote><em>"ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES OBTAINED WITH THE PROCEEDS HEREOF. RECOVERY HEREUNDER BY THE DEBTOR SHALL NOT EXCEED AMOUNTS PAID BY THE DEBTOR HEREUNDER."</em></blockquote>

<p>In plain English: <strong>the lender is on the hook for the installer's fraud.</strong> If Sunrun's salesperson lied about your bill going to zero, the FTC Holder Rule says you can assert that fraud claim against GoodLeap or Mosaic — not just against Sunrun. Recovery is capped at what you've already paid, but that is frequently enough to wipe out the remaining balance entirely.</p>

<p>On March 17, 2026, New York Attorney General Letitia James filed a $275 million lawsuit that named <strong>Solar Mosaic and WebBank as co-defendants</strong> alongside the installer Attyx. This was not coincidental. The AG's office specifically used the Holder Rule framework to establish that the lenders participated in and are liable for the fraudulent scheme. (<a href="/ny-ag-attyx-sunco-275-million-lawsuit-2026">Full NY AG breakdown</a>.)</p>

<p>This is the most powerful tool in the arsenal because it targets the party that actually has the money. Installers go bankrupt; their lenders often don't.</p>

<h2>#2 — TILA Rescission (15 U.S.C. § 1635)</h2>

<p>The Truth in Lending Act gives consumers up to <strong>3 years to rescind</strong> a loan — not just 3 days — if the lender made a material disclosure defect AND the loan is secured by the borrower's principal dwelling.</p>

<p>Material defects that extend the rescission window include: wrong APR disclosed, wrong amount financed, wrong finance charge, missing Notice of Right to Cancel, or defective TILA disclosures at closing. Hidden dealer fees that distort the APR disclosure are a classic trigger.</p>

<p>Here's the nuance most solar exit websites get wrong: <strong>a UCC-1 fixture filing on just the solar equipment does not automatically create a TILA security interest in the home.</strong> The 11th Circuit held in <em>Lankhorst v. Independent Savings Plan Co.</em>, 787 F.3d 1100 (11th Cir. 2015) that if the lender's remedy is limited to repossessing the equipment, the home was never at risk, and TILA's home-protection framework isn't triggered.</p>

<p>TILA rescission DOES apply when the solar loan takes a real security interest in the home — which happens more often than borrowers realize. Solar loans that bundled roof work, electrical panel upgrades, or re-wiring frequently cross into TILA-protected territory. State law (like Massachusetts 209 CMR 32.23) can extend rescission rights even further.</p>

<p>In 2015, the Supreme Court made this cleaner in <em>Jesinoski v. Countrywide Home Loans</em>, 574 U.S. 259 — holding that a written notice to the lender (not a lawsuit) is enough to trigger rescission within the 3-year window. You can rescind by letter. The lender then has to respond.</p>

<h2>#3 — The FTC Cooling-Off Rule (16 C.F.R. Part 429)</h2>

<p>For any in-home sale of $25 or more, federal law gives you <strong>3 business days to cancel, no questions asked</strong>. The clock only starts when the seller provides the required written notice in the required format. <strong>Defective notice means the clock never started.</strong></p>

<p>The defects that keep the cooling-off window open for years:</p>

<ul>
<li>Notice not printed at the required size and format</li>
<li>Notice not provided in the language the sale was conducted in (Spanish pitch with an English notice is defective)</li>
<li>Notice not detachable, not in duplicate, or lacking the required "Buyer's Right to Cancel" heading</li>
<li>No notice provided at all</li>
</ul>

<p>If your cooling-off notice was missing or defective, your 3-day window may still be live — even years after signing. That alone can cancel a contract cold.</p>

<h2>#4 — State Home Solicitation Sales Acts</h2>

<p>Federal law is a floor. Most states have their own home-solicitation law layered on top, often with stronger rights.</p>

<p>California's version (Civil Code §§ 1689.5-1689.14) extends protections and imposes additional disclosure requirements. Some states — Alaska, Arkansas, North Carolina, Wisconsin — extend the cancellation window to 7 days. The same "clock never started" trick from federal cooling-off applies when the state-required disclosures are missing or defective.</p>

<p>Connecticut passed a dedicated solar sales law in 2024 (expanding its existing Home Solicitation Sales Act) that now includes specific requirements for residential solar sales: mandatory identification, training requirements for third-party agents, and restrictions on the hours door-to-door sales can occur. Violations are per-se unfair trade practices.</p>

<h2>#5 — State UDAP and Deceptive Trade Practices Statutes</h2>

<p>Every state has a Unfair and Deceptive Acts and Practices (UDAP) statute. These are the workhorse tools for misrepresentation claims. Each one has teeth:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Texas DTPA</strong> (Bus. &amp; Com. Code § 17.50) — treble damages for knowing violations, plus attorney's fees</li>
<li><strong>California CLRA</strong> (Civ. Code §§ 1750-1784) and <strong>UCL</strong> (Bus. &amp; Prof. § 17200) — actual damages, restitution, injunction, attorney's fees</li>
<li><strong>New York GBL §§ 349 and 350</strong> — treble damages (capped at $1,000 per claim) plus attorney's fees</li>
<li><strong>Florida FDUTPA</strong> (Stat. § 501.201) — actual damages plus attorney's fees</li>
<li><strong>New Jersey Consumer Fraud Act</strong> (NJSA 56:8-1) — <strong>mandatory</strong> treble damages plus attorney's fees</li>
<li><strong>Massachusetts 93A</strong> — double or treble damages for willful violations</li>
<li><strong>Connecticut CUTPA</strong> (Gen. Stat. § 42-110a) — punitive damages plus attorney's fees</li>
</ul>

<p>These stack with federal claims. A salesperson who promised a zero electric bill triggers UDAP in every single state. If your state is one of the ones with treble damages, your case is worth 3x your actual harm — before attorney's fees are added on top. (<a href="/solar-salesman-promised-zero-electric-bill">Full zero-bill misrepresentation guide</a>.)</p>

<h2>#6 — Common-Law Fraudulent Inducement</h2>

<p>Common-law fraud has been around for 400 years. If the salesperson made false statements that induced you to sign, the contract is voidable. Rescission is the remedy. Available in every single state.</p>

<p>The five elements (memorize these):</p>

<ol>
<li>A material misrepresentation of fact (past or present, not future)</li>
<li>Knowledge that it was false, or reckless disregard for truth</li>
<li>Intent to induce reliance</li>
<li>Justifiable reliance by the buyer</li>
<li>Damages</li>
</ol>

<p>"Your electric bill will be zero" is textbook. "You qualify for the full tax credit" when the homeowner obviously has no tax liability is textbook. "You own the system" when it's a lease is textbook. These are material misrepresentations of fact that the salesperson knew or should have known were false.</p>

<p>Common-law fraud is the backup theory for every UDAP claim. When the UDAP statute has a tight technical requirement your case might not quite meet, fraud is usually there to catch it.</p>

<h2>#7 — The Unlicensed Contractor Bar — California B&amp;P § 7031 (THE NUKE)</h2>

<p>If you're a California homeowner, this is the single most powerful tool in your arsenal. It can also be the cleanest — a single facts-check that wipes out the contract entirely.</p>

<p>California Business &amp; Professions Code § 7031 says, in the exact language of the statute:</p>

<blockquote><em>"(a) No person engaged in the business or acting in the capacity of a contractor may bring or maintain any action ... without alleging that they were a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of that act or contract regardless of the merits of the cause of action...</em>

<em>(b) A person who utilizes the services of an unlicensed contractor may bring an action ... to recover all compensation paid to the unlicensed contractor for performance of any act or contract.</em>

<em>(c) A security interest taken to secure any payment for the performance of any act or contract for which a license is required by this chapter is unenforceable if the person performing the act or contract was not a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of the act or contract."</em></blockquote>

<p>Three extraordinary things happen when the installer's license was invalid — expired, suspended, pending, or simply never held — for <strong>even one day</strong> during the install:</p>

<ol>
<li>The installer cannot sue you to collect payment (they lose standing entirely)</li>
<li>You can claw back every dollar you've already paid them</li>
<li>Any security interest — UCC-1, mechanic's lien, any recorded instrument — becomes unenforceable</li>
</ol>

<p>Solar installation requires a C-46 (Solar) or C-10 (Electrical) contractor license in California. The check takes literally two minutes: go to the California Contractors State License Board at cslb.ca.gov, enter the installer's name or license number, and verify the exact license status on the day they performed your install.</p>

<p>If the license was invalid for any reason on that day — even if it was reinstated later — the contract is unenforceable. This is not a technicality. It's the California Supreme Court's position: <em>"California's contractor licensing statutes severely restrict the remedies available to unlicensed contractors."</em></p>

<p>Similar (though not identical) statutes exist in Texas (Tex. Occ. Code §§ 1302, 1303), Florida (Fla. Stat. § 489.128), Nevada (NRS 624.320), and Arizona (A.R.S. § 32-1153).</p>

<h2>#8 — Forgery and Unauthorized E-Signatures</h2>

<p>A forged signature makes a contract <em>void ab initio</em> — treated as if it never legally existed. This is categorically stronger than any misrepresentation claim. You're not arguing the contract was signed based on false statements; you're arguing there is no contract at all.</p>

<p>The ESIGN Act (15 U.S.C. § 7001) and the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (adopted in 49 states) both require genuine intent to sign for an electronic signature to be valid. A forged e-signature doesn't meet the intent requirement. A spouse signing for the other spouse without authorization doesn't meet it. A rep tapping through on an iPad while the homeowner watches without understanding doesn't meet it.</p>

<p>The NY Attorney General's March 17, 2026 complaint against Attyx alleges the company obtained electronic signatures "without first providing the consumer with copies of the agreements they were signing" and that, "on occasion, sales staff simply forged consumers' e-signatures on the agreements." That allegation, if proven, voids every affected contract.</p>

<p>Tablet-based signing workflows are used by Sunrun, Freedom Forever, Momentum Solar, Vivint, and hundreds of smaller dealer-network installers. If your signature on your contract doesn't match your actual signature style — different slant, different spacing, different stroke characteristics — or if initials appear on pages you don't remember seeing, you may have the strongest possible cancellation claim. (<a href="/solar-contract-forged-signature-what-to-do-2026">Full forged signature guide</a>.)</p>

<h2>#9 — Elder Abuse Statutes</h2>

<p>If the homeowner is 65 or older (or a dependent adult) and the solar sale involved misrepresentation or undue influence, damages can be <strong>doubled or tripled</strong> under elder financial abuse statutes. California's Welfare and Institutions Code § 15610.30 is the most powerful in the country.</p>

<p>Elder abuse statutes typically allow:</p>

<ul>
<li>Double or treble compensatory damages</li>
<li>Attorney's fees (usually mandatory)</li>
<li>Punitive damages</li>
<li>A separate private right of action</li>
</ul>

<p>40+ states have similar statutes. The NY Attorney General's complaint against Attyx specifically emphasizes that the company "targeted elderly, low-income consumers." That framing was intentional — under NY GBL and NY elder-abuse law, the damages calculation goes up dramatically when the plaintiffs are seniors.</p>

<p>For homeowners over 65, this means the remedy package is almost always bigger — and the settlement pressure on the defendants is almost always higher — than an equivalent case brought by a younger homeowner.</p>

<h2>#10 — Unconscionability</h2>

<p>Unconscionability is the legal doctrine that lets a court throw out — or rewrite — a contract that is so one-sided it "shocks the conscience." It comes from UCC § 2-302 (for goods contracts) and the Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 208 (for services contracts, which is what solar leases and PPAs typically are).</p>

<p>Two prongs, both usually required:</p>

<p><strong>Procedural unconscionability</strong> — adhesion contract, rushed signing, meaningful choice absent, language barrier, literacy issues, high-pressure sale environment. A 45-minute door-to-door pitch ending in a tablet signature on a 25-year lease is textbook procedural unconscionability.</p>

<p><strong>Substantive unconscionability</strong> — grossly unfair terms. A 2.9% annual escalator clause that takes a $150/month lease to $298/month over 25 years is substantively unconscionable. A buyout formula designed to always price exits above equipment value is substantively unconscionable. (<a href="/is-sunrun-solar-lease-predatory">Full breakdown on the Sunrun lease mechanics</a>.)</p>

<p>When both prongs are met, courts can strike out the offending clause, reform the contract, or void it entirely. Unconscionability is a fallback theory that works even when fraud or misrepresentation can't be proven — it's purely about the unfairness of the contract itself.</p>

<h2>#11 — Failure to Obtain Permission to Operate (PTO)</h2>

<p>Solar panels installed on a roof don't produce power until the utility issues Permission to Operate. Without PTO, the system is dark. Without PTO, the installer hasn't delivered the product the homeowner was sold.</p>

<p>PTO delays of 6+ months are extraordinarily common. Solar loan payments often start as soon as the equipment is installed — meaning homeowners pay for months on a system that has never produced a kilowatt. That's material breach, and material breach is grounds to rescind the contract and attack the loan under the FTC Holder Rule.</p>

<p>If your solar panels are on your roof but you have never received PTO from your utility, you are currently paying for nothing. That's actionable today.</p>

<h2>#12 — State-Specific Solar Statutes</h2>

<p>A growing number of states are passing dedicated solar consumer protection laws on top of general UDAP statutes. Check your state's current activity:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Connecticut</strong> passed SB 297 in 2024 requiring third-party solar agents to identify themselves, be trained by the provider, and honor specific disclosure requirements</li>
<li><strong>California</strong> uses a combination of CLRA, UCL, B&amp;P § 7031, and Civil Code 1689.5 rather than a solar-specific statute</li>
<li><strong>New York</strong> has Public Service Commission licensing requirements (the ones Attyx was revoked under in late 2025)</li>
<li><strong>Texas</strong> uses the DTPA as its primary solar consumer protection mechanism (and the AG is actively investigating Sunrun, Freedom Forever, Lone Star Solar, and CAM Solar under it as of April 6, 2026)</li>
<li><strong>Colorado</strong> uses the Colorado Consumer Protection Act (§ 6-1-105)</li>
</ul>

<p>Active enforcement is the signal that matters most. States with active AG investigations (Texas, New York, Connecticut, California, Colorado, Minnesota) have dramatically shorter windows for individual homeowners to settle on favorable terms.</p>

<h2>#13 — Bankruptcy Contract Rejection (11 U.S.C. § 365)</h2>

<p>When a solar installer files Chapter 11 or Chapter 7 bankruptcy, the installer can "reject" executory contracts — warranty and service agreements that still require future performance. Rejection legally counts as a pre-petition breach.</p>

<p>For homeowners, this means:</p>

<ul>
<li>You have a pre-petition unsecured claim against the bankruptcy estate</li>
<li>You have standing to rescind the underlying PPA or loan as damages</li>
<li>The lender (under the FTC Holder Rule) inherits your claims</li>
</ul>

<p>Sunnova (2024) and SunPower (2024) both went bankrupt. SunStrong Management took over their customer accounts and then explicitly refused to honor the original warranties — stating in a letter <em>"We did not assume Sunnova Production Guarantee or Limited Warranty obligations."</em> Connecticut Attorney General William Tong opened a formal investigation of SunStrong on March 26, 2026. (<a href="/sunstrong-warranty-not-honored-sunnova-sunpower-2026">Full SunStrong breakdown</a>.)</p>

<p>Freedom Forever filed Chapter 11 on April 15, 2026. The same pattern is likely to repeat. If your installer has filed bankruptcy, the combination of § 365 rejection + Holder Rule attack on your loan often results in full loan cancellation. (<a href="/freedom-forever-bankruptcy-2026">Full Freedom Forever breakdown</a>.)</p>

<h2>#14 — Usury Caps</h2>

<p>Some states cap consumer loan interest rates at 18-24% APR. Solar loans with hidden dealer fees often push the effective APR above those caps. When that happens, the loan may be voidable under state usury law.</p>

<p>The complication is federal preemption. Most solar loans are funded by federally chartered banks like WebBank — a Utah-based bank that partners with Solar Mosaic, GoodLeap, and others. Under federal banking law, these banks can export the interest rate of their home state (Utah has no usury cap) to loans nationwide. So state usury caps are often preempted.</p>

<p>When state usury caps MAY still apply: (1) the loan was funded by a state-chartered, non-federally-insured lender; (2) the loan was originated by the installer rather than a bank; (3) the state has specific statutes that extend usury protection despite federal preemption (New York Banking Law §§ 14-a and 108 have partial extensions).</p>

<p>This one rarely applies in isolation but is useful as a supplementary attack when the hidden-dealer-fee APR math is egregious.</p>

<li style="margin:0 0 10px 0;padding-left:8px;">Look for the FTC Holder Rule notice in your loan docs (it's in bold, all caps, and must appear on the face of the agreement).</li>

<li style="margin:0 0 10px 0;padding-left:8px;">For California homeowners: pull the installer's CSLB license status history. Any gap triggers B&P 7031.</li>
<li style="margin:0 0 10px 0;padding-left:8px;">Write down every specific promise your salesperson made verbally. Even handwritten notes matter. That's your misrepresentation foundation.</li>
</ul>

## FAQ

### What is the single most powerful legal tool to cancel a solar contract?

The FTC Holder Rule (16 CFR 433.2) is typically the most powerful because it makes the solar lender — GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Dividend — liable for the installer's fraud. This targets the party that actually has the money. The NY AG specifically used this framework when naming Solar Mosaic and WebBank as co-defendants in the $275M Attyx lawsuit filed March 17, 2026. For California homeowners, Business & Professions Code 7031 is arguably equivalent in power because it bars unlicensed contractors from collecting anything at all.

### Can I cancel a solar contract after 3 years?

Yes, under multiple paths. TILA rescission gives up to 3 years when the loan is secured by your home and the lender made a material disclosure defect. State UDAP claims run from the date you discovered the deception (not when you signed). The FTC Cooling-Off Rule window stays open indefinitely when the required notice was defective — which is extremely common. Common-law fraudulent inducement has no hard deadline. California B&P 7031 has no statute of limitations at all. For homeowners 65 or older, elder abuse statutes extend and enhance these remedies further.

### What is California Business & Professions Code 7031 and why does it matter for solar?

Cal. B&P 7031 says an unlicensed contractor cannot collect payment for work that required a license. Solar installation in California requires a C-46 (Solar) or C-10 (Electrical) license. If the installer's license was invalid — expired, suspended, pending — for even one day during the installation, three things happen: (1) the installer loses standing to sue for payment, (2) the homeowner can claw back every dollar already paid, and (3) any security interest tied to the contract becomes unenforceable. This is the cleanest cancellation path in California because it requires no proof of fraud — just verification of license status on the CSLB website.

### Does my solar loan survive if my installer goes bankrupt?

Legally yes, but bankruptcy actually creates strong grounds to attack the loan. Under 11 USC Section 365, the installer's rejection of warranty and service agreements counts as a pre-petition breach. Under the FTC Holder Rule, those breach claims flow directly to the lender. The installer's inability to deliver the product that justified the loan in the first place often results in full loan cancellation via the Holder Rule — without needing to sue the bankrupt installer. This is why the Freedom Forever Chapter 11 filing of April 15, 2026 and the Sunnova/SunPower 2024 bankruptcies have produced more customer wins, not fewer.

### How many legal loopholes do I need to cancel my solar contract?

One can be enough when the facts are strong — particularly California B&P 7031, forged e-signature, or a clear TILA rescission case. But most full contract cancellations stack 3-4 overlapping claims. A typical strong California case might allege: FTC Holder Rule against the lender, CLRA and UCL misrepresentation against the installer, common-law fraudulent inducement as backup, B&P 7031 if the license was invalid, unconscionability attack on the escalator clause, and elder abuse enhanced damages if the homeowner is 65 or older. Stacking these forces settlement. The best outcome is full contract cancellation with the solar equipment retained.
