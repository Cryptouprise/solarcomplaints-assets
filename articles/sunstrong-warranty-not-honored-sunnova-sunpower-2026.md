<h2>The Legal Theory — Why This Is a Strong Case</h2>

<p>When a company acquires another company's customer accounts and servicing rights, there are legal doctrines that determine what obligations transfer. You do not get to pick and choose — you cannot keep the revenue streams and discard the warranty obligations. That is called "successor liability," and it exists precisely to prevent what SunStrong appears to be doing.</p>

<p>Add to that:</p>

<ul>
<li><strong>Breach of contract claims</strong> — your original contract required specific services that SunStrong is no longer providing</li>

<li><strong>FTC Holder Rule claims against the solar lender</strong> — if your solar loan was financed through Mosaic, GoodLeap, Sunlight, or Dividend, the lender is subject to your claims against the installer/successor under the Holder Rule</li>
<li><strong>Bankruptcy plan violation claims</strong> — Sunnova and SunPower bankruptcy reorganization plans specified what obligations would continue; SunStrong appears to be violating those plans in some cases</li>

### What is SunStrong Management and why did they take over my Sunnova or SunPower account?

### Is SunStrong legally allowed to refuse to honor my Sunnova or SunPower warranty?

That is exactly the question Connecticut Attorney General William Tong opened a formal investigation into on March 26, 2026. Under the doctrine of successor liability, a company that acquires another company's customer accounts and revenue streams generally cannot selectively discard the obligations attached to those accounts. SunStrong's position that they acquired the servicing rights but not the warranties is legally contested.

### SunStrong started charging me $10/month for monitoring. Is that legal?

Your original Sunnova or SunPower contract likely included system monitoring as part of the service — meaning the new $10/month SunStrong fee is a unilateral modification of contract terms, which is generally not enforceable. Over 20 years this fee totals $2,400 per customer, which across the acquired customer base is tens of millions of dollars. Document the fee on your billing statements, pull your original contract showing monitoring was included, and file a complaint with your state AG.

### What does the Connecticut AG SunStrong investigation mean for customers in other states?

The simultaneous $275,000 CT AG settlement with Spruce Power — another solar successor entity — sets a template that pressures SunStrong to settle.

### Do I still have to pay my Sunnova or SunPower solar loan if SunStrong won't honor the warranty?

But under the FTC Holder Rule, your lender is subject to the same claims and defenses you have against the seller — meaning when SunStrong fails to deliver the services (warranty, production guarantee, monitoring) that were the basis of the original loan, you have strong grounds to challenge or cancel the loan itself.
