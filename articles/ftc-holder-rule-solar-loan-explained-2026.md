<h2>What the Holder Rule Actually Says</h2>

<p>The required notice is brief. It must appear in at least 10-point bold-face type on every consumer credit contract that funds a purchase of consumer goods or services. The exact language, taken from 16 C.F.R. § 433.2:</p>

<blockquote><em>"ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES OBTAINED WITH THE PROCEEDS HEREOF. RECOVERY HEREUNDER BY THE DEBTOR SHALL NOT EXCEED AMOUNTS PAID BY THE DEBTOR HEREUNDER."</em></blockquote>

<p>Three things this language does:</p>

<p><strong>1. "Any holder ... is subject to all claims and defenses."</strong> Translation: whoever owns your loan now — even if it has been sold or assigned to a different lender after origination — inherits everything you could have argued against the original installer. If Sunrun's salesperson lied to you, you can assert that lie against GoodLeap. If Freedom Forever failed to deliver a working system, you can assert that failure against Mosaic. The lender does not get to claim "I was a neutral third party, take it up with the installer."</p>

<p>This is a fundamental break from how commercial law normally works. In most contexts, when a debt is sold to a third party, the third party is a "holder in due course" who buys the debt free of the original obligor's claims. The Holder Rule abolishes that protection for consumer credit. The whole point is that consumer-credit financing arrangements between sellers and lenders are not arms-length — they are coordinated schemes — so the law refuses to let lenders hide behind the legal fiction that they did not know what was going on.</p>

<p><strong>2. "Recovery hereunder by the debtor shall not exceed amounts paid by the debtor hereunder."</strong> Translation: the most you can recover from the lender is what you have actually paid them. If you have paid $8,000 on a $50,000 loan, your maximum cash recovery is $8,000 — but importantly, the remaining $42,000 balance is also subject to attack. In practice, the recovery cap is rarely the binding constraint. The bigger value is in canceling future payments and clearing the loan from your credit.</p>

<p><strong>3. The notice has to be there.</strong> The Holder Rule requires the seller (the installer) to ensure that the lender's contract includes the notice. In practice, almost every solar loan from GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Dividend, and Service Finance contains the notice — they include it specifically to comply with the rule and to enable the secondary loan market. Pull your loan documents. Look for the bold notice. It is almost certainly there.</p>

<h2>Why the Holder Rule Was Written</h2>

<p>This part matters because lenders' defenses ignore it.</p>

<p>Before 1975, the standard practice in consumer credit went like this: a fraudulent seller would sell faulty goods or services on credit. The seller would immediately assign that credit contract to a creditor (or arrange a "purchase money loan" from a creditor). The assigned contract would waive the consumer's right to raise seller-related defenses against the assignee. So when the consumer discovered the fraud and stopped paying, the creditor would sue them and win, because the creditor was a "holder in due course" not subject to the seller's misconduct claims.</p>

<p>The FTC studied this for years. They found that the credit assignment system was actively profitable for fraudulent sellers — they could collect their money up front from the lender and walk away from the consumer entirely, leaving the consumer with both faulty goods and a non-defensible loan. The Holder Rule was the FTC's solution: by making the lender's loan subject to the consumer's claims against the seller, the rule eliminated the lender's incentive to fund deals with shady sellers.</p>

<p>The National Consumer Law Center calls the Holder Rule "the most effective action the FTC has ever taken to prevent and remedy consumer fraud." That is not marketing language — it is the considered judgment of the consumer protection bar after 49 years of practice with the rule.</p>

<h2>How It Applies to Solar Loans</h2>

<p>Solar financing is a textbook case of the kind of arrangement the Holder Rule was written for. The standard structure:</p>

<ul>
<li>The installer (Sunrun, Freedom Forever, Momentum, Vivint, Sunnova, etc.) sells the system through door-to-door, in-home, or virtual sales reps</li>
<li>The installer has an enrolled-contractor relationship with one or more lenders (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Dividend, Service Finance)</li>
<li>The installer steers the customer to that specific lender ("we have great financing options")</li>
<li>The lender funds the deal directly, paying the installer the loan proceeds (minus any dealer fee that goes to the lender — see <a href="/hidden-dealer-fee-solar-loan-30-percent-2026">the dealer fee breakdown</a>)</li>
<li>The customer's loan documents include the FTC Holder Rule notice</li>
</ul>

<p>That is the textbook "purchase money loan" arrangement that triggers Holder Rule coverage. The lender is not arms-length. The installer-lender relationship is documented in their enrolled-contractor agreements. The lender knows or should know what kind of sales practices the installer uses, because the lender is funding thousands or millions of those transactions.</p>

<h2>How the Lenders Are Defending Against Holder Rule Claims</h2>

<p>Three defenses, all with serious weaknesses:</p>

<h3>Defense 1: "We are not a 'holder' subject to the rule."</h3>

<p>Some lenders argue that because the loan was technically structured as a "purchase money loan" (where the lender writes a check to the consumer who then pays the installer) rather than as an "assigned contract" (where the seller's contract was sold to the lender), the rule's broader holder protections do not apply.</p>

<p>This defense usually loses. The FTC's own commentary, FDIC's Consumer Compliance Examination Manual, and the great weight of case law treat purchase-money-loan arrangements as falling squarely within Holder Rule coverage when there is a referral or business relationship between the seller and the lender. Solar installers and their preferred lenders almost always have such relationships, documented in enrolled-contractor agreements.</p>

<h3>Defense 2: "The Holder Rule notice was not in the loan documents."</h3>

<p>Some lenders argue that because their specific loan documents did not include the required FTC notice (or included it in defective format), they cannot be held liable as "holders" under the rule. There are two responses to this defense.</p>

<p>First, the absence of the notice does not eliminate the substantive Holder Rule liability — it eliminates the contractual mechanism for asserting it. The "close connection doctrine," recognized by courts including the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court in <em>Ford Motor Credit Co. v. Morgan</em>, 536 N.E.2d 587 (Mass. 1989), holds that lenders who are closely connected to the underlying transaction can be held to Holder Rule-equivalent liability even when the notice was missing.</p>

<p>Second, the absence of the notice was the seller's failure to comply with the rule, which itself is an unfair and deceptive practice. The seller's UDAP violation can be asserted against the lender under state law on a separate theory.</p>

<h3>Defense 3: "Recovery is capped at amounts paid, so cancellation is not available."</h3>

<p>Lenders sometimes argue that because the Holder Rule limits recovery to "amounts paid by the debtor," the consumer can only recover cash already paid — not cancel the remaining loan balance.</p>

<p>This is the most technically debatable defense. Some courts read the Holder Rule narrowly. But the practical answer is that even under the narrow reading, the consumer's defenses (claims arising from the seller's misconduct) can be raised against the lender's collection efforts. So even if the consumer cannot affirmatively recover beyond amounts paid, the consumer can defeat the lender's enforcement of the remaining balance — which produces the same practical result.</p>

<p>And the practical answer is bigger: lenders almost never want a Holder Rule case to go to trial. The discovery is bad for them. The optics are bad for them. The risk of an adverse ruling that establishes broader liability is bad for them. Settlement happens, and settlement typically involves principal reduction or full balance forgiveness, regardless of what the technical recovery cap might allow.</p>

<h2>The Strongest Holder Rule Cases</h2>

<p>From the case patterns I have reviewed, the strongest Holder Rule cases share three or four of these features:</p>

<ol>
<li><strong>Documented installer fraud or material breach</strong> — recorded sales calls, written promises that did not materialize, production data showing significant shortfall, unanswered warranty claims, or the installer's bankruptcy filing itself</li>
<li><strong>The installer is dead, dying, or judgment-proof</strong> — installer bankruptcy, license revocation, or simply being too small to collect from. This forces the consumer to attack the lender, and forces the lender to actually engage with the case</li>
<li><strong>The lender's relationship with the installer is documented</strong> — enrolled-contractor agreements, dealer fee structures, marketing materials promoting the lender's relationship with the installer</li>
<li><strong>State UDAP claims stack on top</strong> — pursuing the lender under both the Holder Rule (federal) and the state UDAP statute (state) creates multiple liability theories. UDAP statutes often add treble damages and attorney's fees that are not available under the Holder Rule alone</li>
</ol>

<h2>The Bigger Picture</h2>

<p>The FTC Holder Rule was written for exactly this moment. Forty-nine years ago, the agency saw that fraudulent sellers would always exist, but that the credit-financing infrastructure that enabled them did not have to. By making lenders share liability for sellers' misconduct, the rule was designed to force lenders to police their own enrolled-seller networks.</p>

<p>The solar industry is the rule's biggest test case in a generation. <p>The lenders who funded the modern solar boom are now learning what consumer protection lawyers have always known: when you fund deals with shady sellers, the Holder Rule eventually catches up to you. And it is catching up now.</p>

## FAQ

### What is the FTC Holder Rule and how does it apply to my solar loan?

The FTC Holder Rule (16 CFR 433.2) is a 49-year-old federal regulation requiring consumer credit contracts to include a notice making the lender liable for the seller's misconduct, up to amounts the consumer has paid. Every consumer credit contract — including solar loans from GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight Financial, Dividend, and Service Finance — must include the notice in 10-point bold-face capital letters. When solar lenders fund installations through enrolled-contractor relationships with installers (Sunrun, Freedom Forever, Momentum, Sunnova, SunPower, Vivint, etc.), the Holder Rule applies: the consumer can assert claims directly against the lender for the installer's fraud, misrepresentation, breach of warranty, failure to deliver, or any other actionable conduct. The National Consumer Law Center calls the Holder Rule 'the most effective action the FTC has ever taken to prevent and remedy consumer fraud.'

### Can I really sue GoodLeap or Mosaic for what my solar installer did?

Yes — under the FTC Holder Rule (16 CFR 433.2), absolutely. The whole point of the rule is to make lenders responsible for the misconduct of the sellers they fund. The New York Attorney General did exactly this on March 17, 2026, naming Solar Mosaic and WebBank as co-defendants in the $275M lawsuit against Attyx. The AG explicitly framed the lenders as joint participants in the fraud, not innocent third parties. For individual consumer cases, the lender's liability is capped at amounts you have paid them — but in practice, settlements typically include balance forgiveness for the remaining loan amount, credit reporting cleanup ('paid in full' rather than 'settled'), and dealer fee disgorgement. The strongest cases stack the Holder Rule claim with state UDAP statutes (which often add treble damages and attorney's fees), TILA disclosure violations, and common-law fraud.

### What if my loan documents don't have the FTC Holder Rule notice?

Two answers. First, almost all major solar lenders (GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Dividend, Service Finance) include the notice in their standard loan documents because they need to comply with the rule to enable the secondary loan market. Pull your documents — the bold capital letter notice is almost certainly there. Second, even if the notice is missing, the 'close connection doctrine' recognized in cases like Ford Motor Credit Co. v. Morgan, 536 N.E.2d 587 (Mass. 1989) holds that lenders who are closely connected to the underlying transaction (through enrolled-contractor agreements, referral arrangements, or marketing relationships) can be held to Holder Rule-equivalent liability even without the notice. The seller's failure to include the required notice is itself an unfair and deceptive practice that supports state UDAP claims against the lender.

### What kinds of installer misconduct trigger Holder Rule liability for the lender?

Any claim or defense the consumer could have asserted against the installer. Examples that have produced successful Holder Rule cases against solar lenders: (1) production well below the sales projection (material breach + misrepresentation); (2) salesperson promised zero electric bill that did not materialize (fraudulent inducement); (3) tax credit eligibility misrepresentation (the salesperson promised the homeowner would 'definitely' get the 30% federal credit when the homeowner had no tax liability); (4) installer bankruptcy and inability to honor warranty (material breach via failure of consideration); (5) hidden dealer fees that distort the disclosed price (misrepresentation about cost of credit); (6) unlicensed contractor performing work that required licensing (fraud + statutory violation); (7) forged e-signatures (contract void ab initio); (8) failure to obtain Permission to Operate (system not delivering promised functionality).

### How much can I actually recover under the FTC Holder Rule?

Statutorily, the recovery cap is 'amounts paid by the debtor' — meaning the cash you have actually paid to the lender to date. If you have paid $8,000 on a $50,000 loan, your statutory cash recovery is $8,000. But in practice, that cap is rarely the binding constraint for two reasons. First, the Holder Rule allows the consumer to raise the seller's misconduct as a defense to the lender's enforcement of the remaining balance — so even if you cannot affirmatively recover beyond amounts paid, you can defeat collection of the remaining $42,000. Second, lenders almost never let Holder Rule cases go to trial — settlement happens. Settlements typically include principal reduction or full balance forgiveness (eliminating the remaining loan), credit reporting cleanup ('paid in full' status), dealer fee disgorgement, and sometimes attorney's fees. The practical recovery is usually multiples of the statutory cap when you account for balance forgiveness.
