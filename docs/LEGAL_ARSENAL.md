### 1. FTC Holder Rule — 16 C.F.R. § 433.2

**What it does:** The lender is liable for the seller's fraud. If GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, Dividend, or Service Finance funded a loan tied to a fraudulent solar sale, the homeowner can assert claims against the LENDER directly — not just the installer.

**Primary source:** [eCFR 16 CFR 433.2](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-16/chapter-I/subchapter-D/part-433/section-433.2)

**The required notice (exact language):**
> ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES OBTAINED WITH THE PROCEEDS HEREOF. RECOVERY HEREUNDER BY THE DEBTOR SHALL NOT EXCEED AMOUNTS PAID BY THE DEBTOR HEREUNDER.

**Live application:** NY AG Letitia James named Solar Mosaic and WebBank as co-defendants in the $275M Attyx lawsuit (March 17, 2026) — establishing that solar lenders are NOT protected when the underlying sale was fraudulent.

**The caveat:** Covers "Purchase Money Loans" — where the lender has a referral or business arrangement with the contractor. Most solar lenders (GreenSky, Mosaic, Service Finance, Sunlight) operate through enrolled contractor networks, which triggers the rule. Recovery capped at amounts already paid.

**Article framing:** "When you financed a solar system through the installer's preferred lender, that lender is legally on the hook for the installer's fraud under a federal rule called the FTC Holder Rule. This is the single most powerful tool in your arsenal."

### 3. FTC Cooling-Off Rule — 16 C.F.R. Part 429

**What it does:** 3 business days to cancel for any in-home sale of $25 or more. The clock only starts when the seller provides the required written notice in the required format. Defective notice = clock never started = cancellation right is still live years later.

**Primary source:** [FTC Cooling-Off Rule](https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/rules/cooling-rule)

**The defects that keep the clock open:**
- Notice not printed at the required size/format
- Notice not in the language the sale was conducted in (Spanish pitch → English notice = defective)
- Notice not detachable or not in duplicate
- Notice missing the required "Buyer's Right to Cancel" heading
- No notice provided at all

**Article framing:** "Federal law gives you 3 days to cancel any door-to-door sale over $25. But here's what they don't tell you: the clock only starts if the notice they gave you was legally correct. If it wasn't, your 3-day window may still be open — even years later."

### 4. State Home Solicitation Sales Acts (CA specifically)

**California Civil Code §§ 1689.5-1689.14**

**What it does:** Layered on top of federal cooling-off. California extends the federal 3-day cancellation right and requires stronger disclosures. Some states extend to 7 days.

**Primary source:** [California Civil Code 1689.5 - leginfo.legislature.ca.gov](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=1689.5.&lawCode=CIV)

**States with 7-day cooling-off on home-solicitation solar sales (verify each when writing):**
- Alaska (AS 45.02.350)
- Arkansas
- North Carolina
- Wisconsin

**Article framing:** "Most states have their own cooling-off law on top of the federal one. California's is codified at Civil Code 1689.5 and gives homeowners even stronger rights than the federal rule."

### 6. Common-Law Fraud / Fraudulent Inducement

**What it does:** If the salesperson made false statements that induced the homeowner to sign, the contract is voidable. Rescission is the remedy. Available in every state.

**The five elements** (memorize these):
1. A material misrepresentation of fact (past or present, not future)
2. Knowledge that it was false (or reckless disregard)
3. Intent to induce reliance
4. Justifiable reliance by the buyer
5. Damages

**Article framing:** "Common-law fraud has been around for 400 years. If the solar salesperson lied about a material fact — savings, tax credit, system performance, lease transferability — and you signed because of that lie, your contract is voidable."

### 7. Unlicensed Contractor Bar — California B&P § 7031 (the NUKE)

**Primary source:** [California Business and Professions Code § 7031](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=BPC&sectionNum=7031.)

**Key language (quote freely):**
> (a) No person engaged in the business or acting in the capacity of a contractor, may bring or maintain any action ... without alleging that they were a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of that act or contract regardless of the merits of the cause of action ...
>
> (b) A person who utilizes the services of an unlicensed contractor may bring an action ... to recover all compensation paid to the unlicensed contractor for performance of any act or contract.
>
> (c) A security interest taken to secure any payment for the performance of any act or contract for which a license is required by this chapter is unenforceable if the person performing the act or contract was not a duly licensed contractor at all times during the performance of the act or contract.

**What this means in plain English:** If the installer's C-46 (Solar) or C-10 (Electrical) license was expired, pending, suspended, or outright invalid for even ONE DAY during the install, the homeowner can:
1. Refuse to pay anything owed
2. Claw back every payment already made
3. Render any associated UCC-1 or mechanic's lien unenforceable

**How to check (include this in every CA article):** Look up the installer at [CSLB License Check](https://www.cslb.ca.gov/OnlineServices/CheckLicenseII/CheckLicense.aspx). Verify the exact license status on the day the work was performed.

**Similar statutes in other states:**
- Texas: Tex. Occ. Code § 1302 (electricians); § 1303 (residential AC/HVAC — some solar work)
- Florida: Fla. Stat. § 489.128
- Nevada: NRS 624.320
- Arizona: A.R.S. § 32-1153

**Article framing:** "If your California solar installer's license was expired for even one day during your install, you may not owe them anything — and you can claw back every dollar you've already paid. California Business and Professions Code Section 7031 is the single most powerful tool in the California homeowner arsenal."

### 8. Forgery / Unauthorized E-Signature

**What it does:** Contract is *void ab initio* (treated as if it never existed). Strongest possible cancellation claim.

**Primary source:** ESIGN Act (15 U.S.C. § 7001) requires genuine intent to sign; UETA (adopted in 49 states) requires same. Forged signatures don't meet the "intent to sign" requirement.

**The NY AG's specific allegation against Attyx:**
> "Attyx repeatedly obtained consumers' electronic signatures… without first providing the consumer with copies of the agreements they were signing"
>
> "on occasion, sales staff simply forged consumers' e-signatures on the agreements"

**Article framing:** "Forged e-signatures aren't a gray area — they make a contract legally nonexistent. The NY Attorney General specifically alleges this happened in the Attyx case. If your signature doesn't look like yours, your contract may have never legally existed."

### 9. Elder Abuse Statutes

**California Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.30** — the strongest in the country.

**Primary source:** [California Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.30](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?sectionNum=15610.30.&lawCode=WIC)

**What triggers enhanced damages:**
- Homeowner is 65+ (or a dependent adult)
- Financial abuse occurred (taking or keeping property by fraud or undue influence)
- Double damages + attorney's fees + punitive damages available

**States with similar statutes (verify each):** NY, FL, TX, NJ, MA, CT, PA, IL, AZ, NV — 40+ total

**Article framing:** "In California, if a solar salesperson targeted a homeowner 65 or older with misleading promises, the homeowner's damages are DOUBLED under the Elder Financial Abuse Act. Similar laws exist in 40+ states."

### 11. Failure to Obtain PTO (Permission to Operate)

**What it does:** If the utility never granted PTO, the system isn't producing. The installer hasn't delivered the product. Material breach = grounds to rescind + FTC Holder Rule attack on the loan.

**How common:** Enormous. Months-long PTO delays are standard — some systems sit inactive for 6+ months waiting on utility approval while loan payments have already started.

**Article framing:** "If your panels are on the roof but your utility never approved them to produce power, you're paying a loan for a product the installer never delivered. That's material breach — and grounds to rescind."

---

### 13. Bankruptcy Contract Rejection — 11 U.S.C. § 365

**What it does:** When an installer files Chapter 11 or Chapter 7 and rejects executory contracts (warranty/service agreements), the homeowner gets a pre-petition breach claim AND standing to rescind the underlying PPA/loan.

**Live application:** Sunnova (2024), SunPower (2024), and Freedom Forever (Chapter 11 filed April 15, 2026) all rejected or will reject customer-facing obligations. SunStrong Management explicitly refused to honor Sunnova and SunPower warranties post-bankruptcy — and Connecticut AG William Tong opened a formal investigation March 26, 2026.

**Primary source:** [11 U.S.C. § 365](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/11/365)

**Article framing:** "When your solar installer files bankruptcy and walks away from their warranty obligations, bankruptcy law gives you a claim — and legal standing to rescind the underlying PPA or loan as damages."

## 🟡 TIER 2 — USE WITH CAREFUL FRAMING

### 2. TILA Rescission — 15 U.S.C. § 1635 (THE NUANCE)

**⚠️ This is where most solar-exit sites get it wrong.** Read carefully.

**The rule:** If the loan is secured by the borrower's principal dwelling AND the lender made a material disclosure defect, the homeowner has a 3-year right to rescind (not just 3 days).

**Material defects that extend the clock to 3 years:**
- Wrong APR disclosed
- Wrong amount financed
- Wrong finance charge
- Missing "Notice of Right to Cancel"
- Missing/defective TILA disclosures at closing

**THE HOLE IN THE COMMON FRAMING:** The 11th Circuit ruled in *Lankhorst v. Independent Savings Plan Co.*, 787 F.3d 1100 (11th Cir. 2015) that **a UCC-1 fixture filing on solar equipment does NOT automatically create a TILA security interest in the home.** The court's reasoning: if the lender's remedy is limited to repossessing the equipment, the home was never at risk, so TILA's home-protection framework isn't triggered.

**When TILA rescission DOES apply to solar:**
- The loan takes an actual deed lien / mortgage on the home (happens more often than borrowers realize)
- The loan includes a home improvement security interest (roof work, panel upgrade, re-wiring)
- State law (e.g., Massachusetts 209 CMR 32.23) extends rescission more broadly than federal TILA
- The solar package was bundled with a larger HELOC or refinance

**Article framing (the right way):**
> "Under the Truth in Lending Act, if your solar loan took a security interest in your home — not just the equipment — and the lender made a material disclosure defect, you have up to 3 YEARS to rescind. The key is checking your loan documents: did the lender take only a UCC-1 on the equipment (limited remedy) or did they take an actual lien on your home (full TILA rescission available)? Solar loans that bundled roof work, panel upgrades, or re-wiring often qualify. Get a case review to find out which category yours falls into."

**Primary sources:**
- [15 U.S.C. § 1635](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/15/1635)
- *Jesinoski v. Countrywide Home Loans*, 574 U.S. 259 (2015) — Supreme Court held written notice alone (not lawsuit filing) is sufficient to rescind within the 3-year window
- *Lankhorst v. Independent Savings Plan Co.*, 787 F.3d 1100 (11th Cir. 2015) — the limiting case

### 10. Unconscionability — UCC § 2-302 / Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 208

**⚠️ The nuance:** UCC § 2-302 applies to "goods." Solar leases and PPAs typically are NOT goods contracts (they're service contracts). So UCC § 2-302 applies by analogy, but common-law unconscionability under Restatement § 208 is the cleaner fit.

**Two prongs (both usually required):**
- **Procedural unconscionability:** Adhesion contract + rushed signing + no meaningful choice + language/literacy issues
- **Substantive unconscionability:** Grossly unfair terms (e.g., 2.9% escalator on a 25-year lease that doubles the payment)

**Article framing:** "A court can throw out — or rewrite — any contract that's procedurally and substantively unfair enough to 'shock the conscience.' That's called unconscionability. Solar leases check both boxes: they're adhesion contracts signed under high pressure with grossly unfair long-term terms."

### 14. Usury Caps

**⚠️ Tricky because of federal preemption.** Most solar loans are funded by federally chartered banks (like WebBank, the Utah-based partner of Solar Mosaic and GoodLeap). Under federal banking law, these institutions can export the interest rate of their HOME state (Utah has no usury cap) to loans nationwide. So state usury caps are often preempted.

**When state usury caps MAY apply:**
- The loan was funded by a state-chartered, non-federally-insured lender
- The loan was originated by the INSTALLER (not a bank)
- The state has specific statutes that extend usury protection despite federal preemption (NY BL §§ 14-a and 108 have some extensions)

**Article framing:** "State usury caps can apply to solar loans in some cases — though federal banking law often preempts them when the lender is a federally chartered bank. If hidden dealer fees pushed your effective APR above your state's legal cap, it's worth a case review to see if your specific loan is subject to state usury law."

## 📚 COMPANION DOCTRINES (Add to Articles as Supporting Authority)

### Rescission in Equity (Common Law)
The flip side of fraudulent inducement. Court can rescind a contract tainted by fraud, mistake, duress, or undue influence. Returns parties to pre-contract position. Available in every state.

### Material Breach Doctrine
If the installer fails to deliver what was promised (wrong system size, wrong equipment, no PTO, unfinished install), that's material breach. Material breach excuses the homeowner from further performance and gives a claim for damages.

### Doctrine of Substantial Nonperformance
A lighter cousin of material breach. When the installer performs, but so poorly that the homeowner didn't get the "benefit of the bargain." Allows reduction of payment.

### Bankruptcy Code Section 365 — Executory Contract Rejection
When an installer files Ch 11/7 and rejects a warranty/service agreement as an "executory contract," the rejection counts as a breach. The homeowner's claim becomes a pre-petition unsecured claim in the bankruptcy AND provides grounds to rescind the underlying PPA/loan.

### Mosaic Bankruptcy Note
Solar Mosaic LLC filed Chapter 11 in June 2025 (separate from Freedom Forever's April 2026 filing). That bankruptcy may affect how Holder Rule claims proceed against them. Check current status before writing.

## 🎯 HOW TO STACK THESE IN AN ARTICLE

The power move is stacking 3-4 claims. A typical strong case against a California Sunrun lease might allege:

1. **FTC Holder Rule** against the lender (Mosaic/GoodLeap)
2. **CLRA + UCL misrepresentation** against Sunrun (the salesperson's "zero bill" promise)
3. **Common-law fraudulent inducement** (as a backup theory)
4. **B&P § 7031** if the installer's license was invalid at any point during install
5. **Unconscionability** attack on the escalator clause
6. **Elder abuse enhanced damages** if the homeowner is 65+

That's six overlapping claims. One might settle the case. Three force dramatic loan reduction. All six usually result in full contract cancellation.

**That stacking pattern is what we teach in our articles. That is Outcome #1.**
